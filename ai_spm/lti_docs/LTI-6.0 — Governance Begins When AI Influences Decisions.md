When AI knowledge starts shaping decisions, governance becomes a PM problem.  
Most AI discussions still focus on outputs:  
model accuracy, hallucinations, prompt quality.  
  
But that framing misses the real issue.  
  
AI does not create risk when it generates text.  
AI creates risk when its knowledge changes behaviour.  
  
Once an AI system starts influencing decisions,  
what to prioritise, what to approve, what to ignore,  
we cross a line.  
  
From that point on, the question is no longer “Is the output correct?”  
The question becomes:  
  
Who is accountable for the consequences of those decisions?  
  
This is where governance begins.  
  
Governance is not about documents, ethics statements, or after-the-fact reviews.  
Governance exists only when someone is explicitly responsible for deciding:  
  
• which knowledge is still allowed to influence action  
• which signals should be downgraded or ignored  
• when a system must stop being trusted  
  
Without a clear decision owner, “AI governance” becomes hindsight justification.  
  
This is also why evaluation alone is not enough.  
  
Evaluation matters only if it can change decisions.  
  
If evaluation results do not alter what the system is allowed to do next,  
they are quality reports, not governance inputs.  
  
In practice, this responsibility does not sit with the model.  
It does not sit with the data.  
It does not sit with the tool.  
  
It sits with the person who decides how AI knowledge is allowed to shape behaviour.  
  
That is a product responsibility.  
  
As AI systems move closer to real decisions,  
governance quietly becomes a PM problem,  
whether the title acknowledges it or not.  
  
Because once AI influences action,  
someone must be trusted to decide when that influence is no longer acceptable.  
  
And that trust is not technical.  
It is a judgement call.
